Deep Research: Human-Agent Interaction

Last Updated: May 2025 | Focus: LLM-based Human-Agent Systems (LLM-HAS)

1. Overview

Human-Agent Interaction (HAI) represents the critical frontier where AI autonomy meets human oversight. While fully autonomous agents promise efficiency, they face reliability challenges (hallucinations, handling complex tasks, safety risks) that limit real-world deployment. LLM-based Human-Agent Systems (LLM-HAS) address these limitations by incorporating human feedback, information, and control into agent workflows.

Key Finding: According to Deloitte's 2026 Global Human Capital Trends, organizations that intentionally design human-AI interactions are 2.5x more likely to report better financial results and 2x more likely to provide meaningful work. Yet only 6% say they're leading in this area.

Nearly 60% of workers now use AI intentionally at work, but few organizations are designing for how humans and machines actually interact. The problem: 59% take a tech-focused approach, layering AI onto legacy systems rather than reimagining collaboration.

2. LLM-HAS Framework (Zou et al., 2025)

The first comprehensive survey of LLM-based Human-Agent Systems identifies five core components:

Environment & Profiling

Task environment definition, agent role specification, capability boundaries, and interaction context setup.

Human Feedback

Feedback types: explicit (corrections), implicit (behavior signals), strategic (goal refinement), operational (execution guidance).

Interaction Types

Supervision, delegation, collaboration, coaching, iterative partnership—each requiring different design patterns.

Orchestration

Task decomposition, role assignment, workflow sequencing, handoff protocols between human and agent.

Communication

Natural language, structured outputs, visual interfaces, multimodal channels (audio, video, text).

3. Interaction Type Taxonomy

Deloitte identifies six distinct interaction types, each requiring specific hardwiring (formal elements) and softwiring (culture, behaviors):

👤→🤖

AI as Tool

Human operates AI like traditional software. Full human agency, AI executes specified commands.

Hardwire: UI design, tool APIs Softwire: Training, familiarity
🤖→👤

AI as Direct Report

Human supervises AI output. AI executes tasks, human reviews and approves. 95% routine automation possible.

Hardwire: Approval workflows, escalation Softwire: Trust building, oversight habits
🤖→👤

AI as Boss

AI directs human work. Algorithms assign tasks, schedule, prioritize. Requires ethical guardrails.

Hardwire: Escalation paths, override rights Softwire: Fairness perception, agency
🤖💡👤

AI as Coach

AI provides guidance and feedback. MetLife's call center coach improved satisfaction 13%, reduced stress.

Hardwire: Feedback channels, metrics Softwire: Learning culture, openness
👥🤖

AI as Collaborator

Iterative partnership. Human and AI work together continuously, each contributing expertise.

Hardwire: Shared context, handoff rules Softwire: Trust, communication
🤖✨👤

AI as Muse

AI sparks creativity. Idea generation, brainstorming, exploration without execution pressure.

Hardwire: Creative interfaces Softwire: Experimentation safety
Case Study: 7-Eleven redesigned recruiter roles when AI automated 95% of routine hiring. Instead of replacing recruiters, they shifted to strategic focus—enabling store leaders, improving quality. Result: elevated talent fit, reduced turnover.

4. Human-in-the-Loop (HITL) Architecture

4.1 Why HITL Matters

Gartner predicts 50% of AI agent deployment failures by 2030 will stem from governance gaps. McKinsey reports 62% experimenting with AI agents, but only 23% scaling in business functions. The gap: insufficient oversight architecture.

4.2 Regulatory Context

  • EU AI Act (August 2026): Article 14 mandates human oversight capabilities for high-risk AI systems
  • CFPB: Lenders must explain AI decisions—unexplainable models cannot be used
  • NIST IR 8596: Requires HITL checks and confidence thresholds for reliability

4.3 Escalation Trigger Design

Effective HITL requires precise criteria distinguishing autonomous execution from human intervention:

Trigger Type Criteria Example Threshold
Confidence Score Model self-reported certainty Below 0.85 for high-risk
Financial Threshold Transaction amount limits Exceeding $10,000
Reputational Risk VIP clients, public-facing Executive review required
Task Complexity Outside training distribution Multi-agent chain > 5 steps
Regulatory Trigger Compliance requirements Healthcare, credit decisions

4.4 Architectural Patterns

Synchronous Approval

Use: Irreversible, high-stakes decisions

Pauses execution pending human authorization. Zero risk of unapproved action but introduces latency.

Examples: Financial transactions, data deletion, account modifications

Asynchronous Audit

Use: Reversible, lower-risk decisions

Execute immediately, log for later review. Near-zero latency but delayed error detection.

Examples: Content classification, recommendations, internal processes

Multi-Tier Oversight

Use: Complex workflows across departments

Planning oversight (strategic) + execution autonomy (tactical). Executive visibility without bottlenecks.

Examples: Multi-department projects, budget planning

5. Trust & Trust Calibration

5.1 The Trust Challenge

Stanford SALT Lab research identifies worker concerns about AI automation:

  • Lack of trust (45%): Workers don't believe AI performs reliably
  • Job replacement fear (23%): Employment security concerns
  • Absence of human touch (16.3%): Tasks requiring interpersonal connection

5.2 Trust Calibration Framework

Research (Lee & See, 2004) establishes: "Trust is better calibrated when systems make their limits as legible as their capabilities." Key principles:

1

Transparency of Limitations

Make uncertainty visible. Show confidence scores, failure modes, boundary conditions.

2

Calibration vs Discrimination

Both must be measured. Calibration: confidence matches success rate. Discrimination: scores separate correct from incorrect outputs.

3

Continuous Feedback Loop

Human corrections improve agent performance over time. Structured feedback feeds into retraining.

4

Verbalized Confidence Issues

LLM self-reported confidence shows systematic overconfidence—a documented reliability limitation.

6. Next-Generation: Interaction Models

Thinking Machines Lab (May 2026) introduced Interaction Models—models that handle interaction natively rather than through external scaffolding. This represents a fundamental shift from turn-based to continuous, real-time interaction.

6.1 The Collaboration Bottleneck

Current models treat autonomy as the primary capability, pushing humans out of the loop. But real work benefits from collaborative process—humans clarify, give feedback along the way. Problem: current interfaces have no room for humans.

6.2 Key Capabilities

  • Seamless dialog management: Model tracks thinking, yielding, self-correction implicitly—no separate dialog component
  • Verbal/visual interjections: Model jumps in as needed, not only when user finishes speaking
  • Simultaneous speech: User and model can speak concurrently (e.g., live translation)
  • Time-awareness: Direct sense of elapsed time
  • Concurrent tool calls: Search, browse, generate UI while speaking/listening

6.3 Architecture: Micro-Turns

Time-aligned micro-turns (200ms chunks) enable near real-time concurrency across modalities. No artificial turn boundaries. The model can:

  • Interrupt when user says something wrong (proactive interjection)
  • React to visual cues (tell me when I've written a bug)
  • Speak while listening (live translation)
  • Watch and commentate (sports game commentary)
Benchmark Results: TML-Interaction-Small achieves best interaction quality while being more intelligent than any non-thinking model. First model with strong intelligence AND interactivity combined.

7. Design Principles (H-ACD Framework)

Human-Agent Centered Design (H-ACD) reframes design practice—not just interfaces, but relationships between people and systems that reason, act, adapt autonomously.

Outcome-Driven

Define human and business outcomes to amplify. Focus on results transcending what humans or AI achieve alone.

Contextual

Tailor solutions to each workflow, team, risk profile, and human-AI relationship. No one-size-fits-all.

Transparent

Make roles, decision rights, trust thresholds, accountability explicit. Everyone understands how contributions combine.

Adaptive

Design for continuous learning, feedback, evolution. Sustain outcomes as needs change.

Human-Centered

Elevate human agency, creativity, judgment, empathy, leadership. AI amplifies, never diminishes human qualities.

Empowering

Workers confident to challenge, escalate, experiment, learn from success and failure.

7.1 Macro vs Micro Design

Effective design operates at two levels:

  • Macro Level: Strategic ambition, governance, design principles, ethics, infrastructure (organization-wide)
  • Micro Level: Work journeys, role definition, human-AI relationship, team composition, performance (task-specific)

8. Policy-Driven Control Architecture

As oversight rules expand, hardcoded checks become brittle. Centralized policy architecture enables fleet-wide governance:

❌ Hardcoded Guardrails

  • Logic in individual agent code
  • Single update requires full redeploy
  • Context compaction can remove rules
  • Prompt injection vulnerability

✅ Centralized Policies

  • Policy changes without app redeploy
  • Fleet-wide governance manageable
  • Policies stored on server (not in context)
  • Separation: devs own hooks, compliance owns rules

Example: @control() decorator turns any function into governed decision point. Compliance updates PII policy across every agent with single change—no code updates, no redeployment, no restarts.

9. Feedback Mechanisms

9.1 Feedback Types (IJCAI 2025 Survey)

Explicit Feedback

Direct corrections, approvals, rejections. User actively provides guidance.

Example: "Change this to X"

Implicit Feedback

Behavior signals: time spent, scroll patterns, acceptance rate. No active user input.

Example: User ignores suggestion → low confidence

Strategic Feedback

Goal refinement, priority adjustment. Shapes agent's understanding of objectives.

Example: "Focus on accuracy over speed"

Operational Feedback

Execution guidance, step-by-step intervention during task execution.

Example: HAC policy model determines intervention timing

9.2 Closing the Feedback Loop

Human corrections must systematically improve performance, not just fix individual errors:

  • Standardized interfaces for reviewer reasoning
  • Categorical feedback enabling pattern analysis
  • Automated integration into retraining workflows
  • Continuous calibration based on production data

11. Challenges & Open Problems

  • Non-determinism violation: Trust requires predictable behavior; agents act probabilistically
  • Confidence calibration: LLM self-reported confidence systematically overconfident
  • Worker resistance: 45% lack trust, 23% fear job replacement
  • Hidden consequences: Shifting routine tasks leaves humans with more complex, demanding work
  • Worker isolation: AI replacing peer collaboration
  • Long sessions: Continuous interaction accumulates context—memory management challenges
  • Compute/deployment: Streaming audio/video requires reliable connectivity
  • Safety: Real-time interfaces stress safety differently than turn-based

12. Future Directions

  • Native interaction models: Interactivity scales with intelligence, not bolted-on harnesses
  • Multimodal collaboration: Audio, video, text in continuous two-way exchange
  • Visual proactivity: Agents respond to visual world changes, not just audio cues
  • Agency-preserving control: Human autonomy protected while leveraging AI capabilities
  • Explainability integration: Interaction-centric explanations, not post-hoc XAI
  • Standardized benchmarks: FD-bench, TimeSpeak, CueSpeak for interaction quality
  • Cross-functional governance: IT, HR, legal, operations as unified oversight
